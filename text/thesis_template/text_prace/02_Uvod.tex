\chapter*{Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Introduction}

\shorthandoff{-}

Machine learning has become a transformative force across the life sciences.
In medical imaging, deep convolutional networks match or exceed expert performance on
diagnostic tasks that were previously the exclusive domain of trained clinicians
\cite{greenerGuideMachineLearning2021}.
In structural biology, the application of attention-based deep learning to the protein
folding problem --- a challenge that had resisted computational solution for half a century
--- produced AlphaFold~2, whose predictions approach experimental accuracy across the
breadth of known protein sequence space \cite{jumperHighlyAccurateProtein2021}.
In drug discovery, generative models now propose candidate molecules with specified
physicochemical properties at a scale and speed inaccessible to traditional screening
approaches.
Across these domains, a common theme emerges: models trained on large corpora of
biological data, without task-specific supervision, acquire representations that are useful
far beyond the immediate training objective.

Nucleotide sequence is a natural substrate for this class of approach.
The architecture of transformer-based large language models \cite{vaswaniAttentionAllYou2017},
developed originally for natural language, transfers to biological sequences in a
conceptually straightforward way: tokens are nucleotides or amino acids rather than words.
Applied to biological sequence, the approach produces models that learn representations
without the constraints of expert-defined features --- the sequence itself provides the
supervision signal.
Whether, and to what degree, such representations capture biologically interpretable
structure in the sequence is an open question that motivates a growing body of
descriptive work.

This thesis contributes to that body of work.
The specific context is transcription factor binding: a well-characterised biological
phenomenon for which large-scale, standardised genomic data are available, and for which
recent descriptive analyses have identified sequence signals extending well beyond the
spatial scale addressed by conventional computational models
\cite{faltejskovaNonconsensusFlankingSequence2026a}.
The approach taken here is descriptive: Evo~2 embeddings \cite{brixiGenomeModellingDesign2026}
are extracted from 10~kb windows centred on ChIP-seq binding sites and examined for
structure that corresponds to known sequence properties of those regions.
