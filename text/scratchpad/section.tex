\section{Global mean-pool embeddings}
\label{sec:results-block1}

The first analysis examines what information is present in a simple global summary of the per-peak embedding: the mean of the 200 binned position vectors, yielding one (4096)-dimensional vector per peak. Principal component analysis of the stacked mean-pool matrix (49\,497 peaks~$\times$~4096 dimensions) concentrates most variance into very few components.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{text_prace/img/block1_pca_umap.png}
    % TODO: regenerate from notebook with final balanced dataset
    \caption{
        Global mean-pool PCA and UMAP of Evo~2 embeddings ($n = 49{,}497$ peaks).
        Top row: PCA scores coloured by transcription factor identity,
        cell line, BED file, and per-peak mean GC content (left to right).
        Bottom row: UMAP ($n_\text{neighbors} = 30$, $\text{min\_dist} = 0.1$)
        coloured by the same variables.
    }
    \label{fig:block1-pca-umap}
\end{figure}

The top five components account for 95.1\% of the total variance (60.9\%, 24.9\%, 4.7\%, 2.2\%, and 0.2\% respectively).
The embedding representation is therefore strongly low-dimensional in practice, consistent with the finding that only 83 components (out of 4096) explain effectively all variance at the $10^{-6}$ threshold.

The first principal component (PC1, 60.9\% of variance) is dominated
by the L2 norm of the mean-pool vector ($r = -0.995$).
Variation in embedding magnitude of this scale is a representational
property of the model rather than a direct reflection of the input
sequence; its biological interpretation is not straightforward.
The small but non-zero effect of transcription factor identity on PC1
($\eta^2 = 0.023$, one-way ANOVA) and of BED file identity
($\eta^2 = 0.032$) are consistent with between-file differences in
mean embedding magnitude, though a contribution from genomic
background differences between datasets cannot be excluded.

The second principal component (PC2, 24.9\% of variance) is strongly
correlated with per-peak mean GC content ($r = +0.877$), identifying
GC composition as the dominant biologically interpretable axis in the
global mean-pool representation.
Transcription factor identity explains 0.6\% of PC2 variance
($\eta^2 = 0.006$).
Coloured by TF, cell line, or BED file, peaks from all groups overlap
throughout the PC1--PC2 plane without visible separation
(Figure~\ref{fig:block1-pca-umap}, top row).
UMAP produces a single connected manifold with a smooth GC gradient
and no discrete structure attributable to TF identity, cell line,
or file (Figure~\ref{fig:block1-pca-umap}, bottom row).

Global mean pooling discards within-window positional structure by
construction.
The absence of TF-discriminating signal in these representations
therefore does not preclude its presence in positional embeddings,
which are examined in the following section.

The Evo~2 7B model generates embeddings autoregressively: each token's
representation is conditioned only on tokens to its left.
Tokens near the left edge of the input window therefore have little or
no left context and produce activations that differ systematically from
those at interior positions. Inclusion of the BOS token had minimal effect on alleviating this effect.
In the untrimmed positional PCA, PC3 exhibits a large spike confined
to the first approximately five bins at the left window edge
($-5000$ to $-4750$~bp), with no corresponding feature at the right
edge (Figure~\ref{fig:block2-untrimmed}).
This left to right asymmetry is the diagnostic signature of the artifact.
The first 15 bins (750~bp) were excluded from all subsequent positional
PCA fits.

\section{Positional embedding profiles}
\label{sec:results-block2}

The global mean-pool analysis collapses the spatial dimension of the embedding, discarding any information about where within the 10\,kb window a given pattern appears.
The positional analysis retains this dimension by computing, for each file, the mean binned embedding averaged over peaks, and then applying PCA to the stacked $(10 \times 200, 4096)$ matrix of file-level profiles.

\subsection{Autoregressive warm-up}

The untrimmed positional PCA immediately reveals a methodological feature of autoregressive models.
PC1 (59.6\% of variance in the untrimmed fit) shows a sharp spike confined to bin~0 -- the first 50\,bp -- that is present in all ten files and absent at the right edge of the window (bin~199).
This asymmetry is the expected signature of the causal context deficit described in Section~\ref{sec:embedding-extraction}: tokens at the start of the input have no left context, while tokens at the right edge have 9950\,bp of preceding sequence available.
The spike is not a sequence-biological signal and must be excluded before interpreting positional structure. % consider hedging a little more

A 5-bin trim (250\,bp) removes the initial spike but a residual warm-up tail remains visible in several files through approximately bin~15. % as commented in methods
A 15-bin trim (750\,bp) is adopted as the working choice; the 5-bin trimmed fit is retained in the notebook for comparison. % maybe add as appendix
All positional findings reported below use the 15-bin trimmed profiles (bins 15--199, covering a 9250\,bp window centred on the binding site).

\subsection{Leading positional components are generic, not TF-specific}

After trimming, PCA on the ten file-level profiles yields PC1 at 66.8\%, PC2 at 20.9\%, and PC3 at 9.1\%.
PC4 accounts for only 0.8\%, indicating that the ten file-average profiles are well described by three components.

PC1 separates the random-windows control from all nine ChIP-seq files at every positional bin. 
Among ChIP-seq files, the ordering along PC1 is graded and consistent with embedding magnitude: CTCF K562 is lowest, MYC GM12878 is highest.
This separation of the random control from peak-centred sequences reflects sequence composition differences -- random windows have a lower average GC content (mean~0.40) than ChIP-seq peak windows (mean~0.43--0.45 by TF) -- rather than a TF-specific binding signal. % but what would that look like?

PC2 shows a smooth left-to-right ramp along the positional axis that is shared across all ten files, including the random-windows control.

\section{Synthesis and limitations}
\label{sec:synthesis}

\subsection{Answers to the research questions}

The three research questions posed in Chapter~\ref{ch:aims} can now be answered directly.

\textbf{Q1: Do the embeddings reflect sequence composition?} % dont like the formatting
Yes, strongly.
PC2 of the global mean-pool PCA is correlated with per-peak GC content at $r = +0.922$, and LDA confirms that GC content is the dominant source of any between-TF signal in the mean-pool representation.
The finding is robust across all 76\,598 peaks and all ten files. % again - distinction of the two analyses

\textbf{Q2: Do they reflect spatial structure beyond overall composition?}
Not detectably, in the analyses performed here.
The leading positional PCs after both magnitude and GC residualisation are shared across all ten files including the random-windows control.
No component examined separates TF binding sites from random windows or separates TFs from one another in a way that is not accounted for by magnitude or GC content. % is the GC content not spatial structre?

\textbf{Q3: Do they distinguish bound from random windows, or different TFs from one another?} % again, "bound" is not good
Partially, but only through GC content.
The random-windows control separates from all ChIP-seq files on PC1 of the positional analysis, reflecting the lower GC content of random genomic windows relative to peak-centred windows. % so a GC matched control would be nice after all? but it is outside the scope
No TF-specific separation is observed. % should probably try a bit harder on the positional analysis, maybe MYC
LDA on the mean-pool embeddings does not discriminate TFs beyond the GC baseline.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations of the analysis are worth making explicit.

\textit{Cell-line coverage.}
The intended balanced design (GM12878, H1, K562 for each TF) was not fully realised: MYC K562 was not embedded within the available compute window and MCF-7 was substituted as the third MYC experiment. % retired design
The resulting imbalance means that any TF--cell-line interaction analysis is confounded, and results for MYC should be interpreted with the substitution in mind. % can this be softened? maybe its contrast. why would the balanced design be any better? 

\textit{Random-windows control.}
The control comprises 2000 windows, smaller than the approximately 9000 peaks per file in the ChIP-seq set.
SPI1 peaks were not excluded from the rejection sampling, leaving a small residual probability of overlap with SPI1 binding sites.

\textit{Autoregressive warm-up.}
The 15-bin (750\,bp) trim adopted to exclude the warm-up artifact reduces the analysable window to 9250\,bp.
Left-context padding, prepending a fixed flanking reference sequence to each input, would eliminate the artifact at source without discarding data but was not retrofitted to the existing embedded set. % but the ends are background, maybe drop from both ends

\textit{File-mean positional analysis.}
The positional PCA is performed on per-file mean profiles, averaging over all peaks within each file.
Per-peak positional analyses, which would retain within-file variation, were not performed and represent a natural extension of the work. % oh i should do that, i have the data

\textit{Single model size and single layer.}
Only the 7B checkpoint and a single intermediate layer (\texttt{blocks.28.mlp.l3}) were examined. % recommended by evo 2 authors. a second layer would be nice
Whether results generalise to other layers or to the 40B checkpoint, which requires hardware not available on CERIT-SC, is unknown. % hardware is available, the choice was deliberate, but how?
