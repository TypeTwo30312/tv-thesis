# Kapitola 1

Transcription factors are sequence-specific DNA-binding proteins that regulate gene expression by recruiting or blocking the transcriptional machinery at target loci. The human genome encodes approximately 1,600 transcription factors [REF], which collectively control when, where, and at what level each gene is expressed. Despite their functional diversity, most transcription factors share a modular architecture: a DNA-binding domain that makes sequence-specific contacts with DNA, and one or more effector domains that interact with cofactors, chromatin remodellers, or components of the core transcriptional machinery.
DNA-binding domains fall into a small number of structural families — zinc fingers, basic helix-loop-helix, basic leucine zipper, homeodomain, and winged-helix, among others — each making characteristic contacts with the major or minor groove [@lambertHumanTranscriptionFactors2018]. The sequence preferences of a given domain are typically summarised as a position frequency matrix, or motif, derived from collections of bound sequences. Motifs capture the information content at each position of the binding site and provide a compact description of which sequences are recognised with high affinity in vitro.
Binding site motifs are typically 6 to 20 base pairs and degenerate, tolerating substitutions at many positions. As a result, high-scoring motif matches are extraordinarily abundant: the human genome contains on the order of hundreds of thousands to millions of candidate sites for most transcription factors [REF]. ChIP-seq [ZKR] experiments, which identify where a factor is actually bound in living cells, typically report between a few thousand and tens of thousands of occupied sites [REF]. Bound sites represent a small fraction — often well below one percent — of all sequences that satisfy the motif. Motif presence is necessary, but it is not sufficient for prediction of binding. These facts contribute to what is known as the transcription factor specificity paradox [@kribelbauerLowAffinityBindingSites2019].
